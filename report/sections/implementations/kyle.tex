The \textbf{ELECTRA model} was implemented using the standard \textbf{AutoModelForSequenceClassification} and \textbf{AutoTokenizer} from Hugging Face. Due to its \textbf{computational efficiency}, we were able to maximize the \textbf{sequence length to ELECTRA’s 512-token limit} without surpassing available compute. This was clearly a helpful setting as it \textbf{outperformed all baselines and models by other groups}, making it an effective improvement by giving the model access to the \textbf{largest possible textual context} during fine-tuning.

The \textbf{loss function} and \textbf{optimizer} were kept at their default settings to isolate the \textbf{architectural benefits of ELECTRA} in comparison to other models. Thus, \textbf{cross-entropy loss} was used as the objective function, which calculates the difference between the \textbf{predicted probability distribution} and the \textbf{ground-truth label}, heavily penalizing the model for being confidently incorrect. The optimizer was \textbf{Adam with Weight Decay (AdamW)}, which pushes the weights toward zero during training to ensure the model remains \textbf{generalized} and reduces the risk of \textbf{overfitting}. Finally, because this implementation uses a \textbf{standard pretrained transformer architecture}, \textbf{feature ablations were not directly applicable} to this specific model.

For model fine-tuning, the parameters involved \textbf{5 epochs}, \textbf{input batch size: 16}, \textbf{learning rate: 0.00002}, and \textbf{weight decay: 0.01}. As a result, the final model achieved an \textbf{Accuracy and Macro F1 score of 86.65\%} (\textbf{Accuracy: 0.8665, Macro-F1: 0.8665}) using the test set. Based on the prior baseline performances (baseline performances in Table 1), the \textbf{ELECTRA model outperforms the majority baseline} and \textbf{slightly improves upon the LR + TF--IDF baseline}.
