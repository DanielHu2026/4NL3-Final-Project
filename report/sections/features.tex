All approaches use the same raw input field, \texttt{lyrics} (free text), and predict one of two labels: \texttt{p} (Pop) or \texttt{m} (Metal). We varied our experiments primarily by changing the \emph{representation} of the lyrics that is fed into the classifier, ranging from sparse bag-of-words features with explicit feature engineering to end-to-end learned contextual representations from pretrained transformer sequence classifiers.

\subsubsection{Feature-engineered Logistic Regression (our main classical model)}
Our strongest non-neural model is a logistic regression classifier trained on a concatenation of three feature blocks: \textbf{(i) word-level TF--IDF} features (unigrams+bigrams), \textbf{(ii) character-level TF--IDF} features (3--6 character n-grams within word boundaries), and \textbf{(iii) numeric lyric-structure features}. The word TF--IDF block captures genre-indicative words and short phrases; we removed English stopwords and performed feature selection implicitly through TF--IDF vocabulary pruning and capping (e.g., \texttt{min\_df=2}, \texttt{max\_df=0.9}, \texttt{max\_features=50000}), and used \texttt{sublinear\_tf=True} to reduce the influence of repeated choruses. The character TF--IDF block (\texttt{char\_wb}, 3--6 grams) was added to capture stylistic and subword cues common in lyrics, such as elongated spellings (e.g., ``oooh''), morphology (``scream/screaming''), and orthographic variation that word tokenization may miss. Finally, we appended standardized numeric features summarizing lyric format and style: word count, unique word count, character count, line count, bracket-tag frequency (e.g., \texttt{[chorus]}), punctuation counts (\texttt{!}, \texttt{?}), all-caps word count, and type--token ratio (lexical diversity). We varied this model across experiments by (a) changing the TF--IDF n-gram settings (e.g., word unigrams vs.\ unigrams+bigrams), (b) adding the character TF--IDF block on top of word TF--IDF, (c) introducing pruning choices such as \texttt{max\_df}, and (d) tuning the logistic regression regularization strength via $C$ (e.g., testing values such as $C \in \{2,4,8,12\}$ and selecting the best on validation).

\subsection{Pretrained transformer sequence classifiers - Models 1 to 3 (contextual representation learning)}
In addition to sparse TF--IDF representations, we trained three pretrained transformer sequence classifiers, which learn contextual embeddings internally and fine-tune them end-to-end for our binary genre task. In each case, the lyrics are tokenized using the model’s pretrained tokenizer, truncated to a fixed maximum sequence length, and passed through a transformer encoder plus a learned classification head (two output logits for \texttt{p/m}). The three transformer backbones we experimented with were \textbf{DistilBERT} (\texttt{distilbert-base-uncased}, max length 256, trained for 2 epochs with learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$ and weight decay 0.01),  \textbf{ELECTRA} (\texttt{google/electra-small-discriminator}, max length 512, trained for 5 epochs with learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$ and weight decay 0.01), and \textbf{XLNet} (\texttt{xlnet-base-cased}, max length 128, trained for 3 epochs with learning rate $2\times10^{-5}$). Across these transformer experiments, the main ``feature variation'' comes from the choice of pretrained backbone and tokenization/truncation length (i.e., the learned representations are model-internal rather than hand-engineered), whereas the feature-engineered logistic regression experiments vary explicit input feature blocks and hyperparameters.

\subsection{Summary of Feature Variation}
Overall, our experimental variations reflect a progression from (1) sparse lexical content features (word TF--IDF), to (2) richer surface-form/style features (character TF--IDF + numeric structure features), and finally to (3) contextual representation learning (transformer sequence classifiers). For the feature-engineered logistic model specifically, we treated feature blocks (word TF--IDF, char TF--IDF, numeric features) as modular components and tuned $C$ to control regularization strength, allowing us to quantify how each feature choice affected validation performance.
